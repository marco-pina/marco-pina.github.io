How does the currency composition of sovereign debt shape the effectiveness of central bank communication? Emerging market economies have successfully "redeemed" Original Sin by shifting external liabilities into local currency, but this transition has introduced a new friction: the "Original Sin Redux." We address this trade-off theoretically and empirically. We introduce a signaling framework where foreign ownership of local currency debt amplifies the sovereign’s incentive to dilute liabilities via surprise inflation. The model predicts that this "debasement risk" erodes the credibility of monetary policy: hawkish communication effectively signals commitment when foreign ownership is low, but is discounted as cheap talk when foreign ownership is high. We test these predictions using a novel dataset of central bank speeches from 15 emerging economies, scored using a transformer-based language model (FOMC-RoBERTa), and measuring credit risk via the Du-Schreger local currency spread. The evidence strongly supports the model: a one-standard-deviation increase in hawkishness reduces credit spreads by approximately 60 basis points in the baseline, but this effect attenuates as foreign participation rises, vanishing entirely when foreign ownership exceeds 77\%. These results highlight an underappreciated cost of local currency debt: while it stabilizes the balance sheet, it can undermine the very credibility that makes cheap borrowing possible.
